Some Basic Information about BJJ

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is a grappling-based martial art whose central theme is the skill of controlling a resisting opponent in ways that force them to submit. The majority of fights end up on the ground, so much of the technique of BJJ is centered around the skill of taking an opponent down to the ground and wrestling for dominant control positions from where the opponent can be subdued. To control and overcome greater size and strength with lesser size and strength is the keynote of the sport. This is done by utilizing superior leverage, grip, and positioning. Students of the sport gain a deep understanding of the workings and limits of the human body.

Below is a table with five basic techniques that a BJJ beginner would expect to learn within the first couple of weeks. The Cross Collar Choke used to be my favorite before I started practicing no gi.

Technique Description Why Use It? Common Mistake
Hip Escape When you're pinned in side control, bridge up then move your hips back to escape side control, get your opponent in guard if possible. You're constantly caught in side control. Hip escapes are easy to learn and very commonly used. Trying to bench press your opponent off of you, relying on brute strength.
Scissor Sweep From guard, drag opponent toward you while sweeping their leg out from under them, then roll on top of them landing in mount. An easy sweep that puts you in a dominant position. Can catch beginners by surprise. Trying to sweep when your opponent's weight is shifted all the way back.
Triangle Choke Choking your opponent by trapping one of their arms between your legs and their own head. This is a classic and powerful choke that can be set up many different ways. Learning the basic setup from guard will allow you to utilize more complicated setups later on. Attacking when your opponent has strong posture.
Cross Collar Choke Placing your hands deep into the collar of the opponent's gi in an X shape, then executing a choke. One of the easiest and most common chokes. The setup for it can lead into many other techniques. Not reaching deep enough into the opponent's collar.
Roll Escape from Mount From a mounted position, trap your opponent's arm and leg, bridge your back, then roll them over. An easy way to escape a very bad position: being mounted. Not trapping the opponent's arm which allows them to post and resist the roll.
